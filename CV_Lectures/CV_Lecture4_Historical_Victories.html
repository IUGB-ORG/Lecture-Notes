Before the Revolution

· · ·

For most of the 20th century, teaching a machine to recognize an image was an unsolved problem that frustrated the best researchers in the field. The dominant approach — hand-engineering features like SIFT, HOG, and Gabor filters, then feeding them to classical classifiers — produced incremental gains but hit a ceiling. Computers could barely tell a cat from a dog. The gap between machine and human vision felt insurmountable.

The story of how that gap closed is not a smooth curve of steady progress. It is a story of three decisive moments — separated by years of quiet work — each of which changed what the field believed was possible.

"The hand-engineering of features is the bottleneck. If we can learn the features, we can learn everything." — The thesis underlying deep learning, 1980s–2010s

Three Moments That Changed Everything

· · ·
1998
LeNet-5 — The Proof of Concept
Yann LeCun and colleagues at Bell Labs deploy the first practical CNN. LeNet-5 reads handwritten ZIP codes on US mail at 98.9% accuracy — at scale, in production. For the first time, a neural network beats hand-engineered systems at a real-world visual task.
1998 – 2011
The Long Wait
CNNs work on small problems but fail to scale. SVMs and random forests dominate computer vision research. Deep learning enters a "winter" — underfunded, underestimated, kept alive by a handful of researchers including LeCun, Hinton, and Bengio.
2012
AlexNet — The Watershed
Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton win ImageNet with a top-5 error of 15.3% — shattering the previous record of 26.2%. The margin is so large that competitors immediately abandon their approaches and begin building CNNs. Deep learning is born as a mainstream discipline.
2013
Dogs vs. Cats — The Democratization
Microsoft sponsors a Kaggle competition: classify 25,000 images of cats and dogs. CNN-based entries achieve >99% accuracy. The competition introduces CNN-based vision to thousands of practitioners worldwide and becomes the canonical "first CNN project" for a generation of engineers.
ImageNet top-5 error rate, 2010–2015. The 2012 drop was the largest single-year improvement in the competition's history.

1998 — LeNet-5 & MNIST

· · ·

Yann LeCun spent the late 1980s and 1990s arguing that the right way to do image recognition was to let the network learn its own features through gradient descent — rather than hand-engineer them. His 1989 paper on backpropagation in convolutional networks was largely ignored. His 1998 paper, "Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition," was not.

The paper introduced LeNet-5 and the MNIST dataset — 60,000 handwritten digit images (28×28 pixels, grayscale) that became the "Hello, World" of machine learning for the next two decades. More importantly, it demonstrated LeNet-5 running on real US Postal Service mail — an industrial deployment of a CNN at scale.

💡 Why MNIST Still Matters

MNIST remains the standard benchmarking dataset for new network architectures. If your architecture cannot reach >99% on MNIST, something is fundamentally wrong. It is simple enough to train in minutes but complex enough to distinguish good designs from bad ones.

🔑 What LeNet Proved

Three things: (1) Learned features are better than hand-crafted ones. (2) The same architecture generalizes across the entire distribution of handwriting styles — one model, all writers. (3) CNNs are deployable in production at scale. The postal system was processing millions of envelopes per day.

The Long Wait — Why Did It Take 14 Years?

· · ·

LeNet worked beautifully on digits. But when researchers tried to scale it to larger, more complex images, it failed — and the AI community largely concluded that neural networks were too expensive and unstable to be practical for real visual tasks. Three structural barriers explain the delay.

🖥️ Barrier 1 — Compute

Training a network on millions of images required hardware that did not exist. A CPU-based training run on ImageNet would take weeks. GPUs capable of parallelizing the matrix operations didn't become programmable for general computation until CUDA was released by NVIDIA in 2007.

💾 Barrier 2 — Data

Neural networks are data-hungry. MNIST has 60,000 images — enough for digits. Real-world vision requires millions of diverse examples. The ImageNet dataset (14M+ labeled images, 1,000 categories) was only assembled and released in 2009 by Fei-Fei Li's group at Stanford.

⚡ Barrier 3 — Algorithms

Deeper networks suffered vanishing gradients. Training reliably required ReLU activations (widespread by 2010), Dropout regularization (Hinton, 2012), and better weight initialization schemes — all of which were unavailable or unappreciated in 1998.

"Everyone in the field thought neural networks were dead. Then AlexNet happened." — Commonly expressed sentiment in retrospective accounts, 2012

2012 — AlexNet & the ImageNet Revolution

· · ·

The ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) was launched in 2010 as the hardest image recognition benchmark ever created — 1.2 million training images, 1,000 object categories, ranging from dogs and cats to specific breeds and species. In 2010 and 2011 the winning methods used hand-engineered features combined with SVMs and ensemble methods, achieving top-5 error rates of 28.2% and 25.8% respectively.

In 2012 a team of three from the University of Toronto — PhD student Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and their supervisor Geoffrey Hinton — entered a deep convolutional neural network they called AlexNet. Their top-5 error: 15.3%. The second-place team: 26.2%. The gap was not an improvement. It was a demolition.

Why AlexNet Won

InnovationWhy it mattered
GPU trainingTrained on 2× NVIDIA GTX 580 GPUs — first major CNN to use GPU parallelism at scale. Reduced training from weeks to days.
ReLU activationsReplaced tanh/sigmoid. Solved vanishing gradients, enabling deeper networks to train stably.
Dropout (p=0.5)Applied after the two large FC layers. Halved overfitting. Network generalized from training images to test images far better.

2013 — Dogs vs. Cats & the Democratization

· · ·

AlexNet proved deep learning worked at the research frontier. But in 2012, training a CNN still required significant expertise — CUDA programming, careful hyperparameter tuning, expensive hardware. The Kaggle platform changed that.

In 2013, Microsoft Research sponsored a Kaggle competition: given 25,000 labeled images of cats and dogs (12,500 each), build a binary classifier. The dataset, contributed by Petfinder.com, contained diverse real-world photographs — different breeds, poses, lighting conditions, and backgrounds. No carefully centered, uniform backgrounds like MNIST.

🌍 Why This Matters

The Dogs vs. Cats competition introduced CNN-based computer vision to tens of thousands of practitioners who were not deep learning researchers. It became the canonical entry-point project — the same role that "Titanic survival prediction" plays for tabular ML. Every major tutorial on CNNs still uses it today.

A CNN's decision boundary for cats vs. dogs. After training, the network separates the two classes with a margin that is nearly impossible to cross.
📅 Chronological Correction

The Dogs vs. Cats competition ran in 2013 — after AlexNet's 2012 victory, not before. AlexNet is what made >99% accuracy on this task achievable in the first place. The competition was designed to let practitioners experience that capability firsthand.

LeNet-5 (1998)

INPUT: 32×32×1 2 CONV LAYERS ~60K PARAMS 99.3% on MNIST

LeNet-5 introduced the core CNN recipe: alternating convolution and pooling layers, followed by fully-connected layers and a classifier. Its key innovation was parameter sharing — the same filter weights applied across the entire image — which dramatically reduced the number of parameters compared to a dense network.

Activation: tanh (sigmoid in the final layer). No Dropout. No data augmentation. Trained on a single CPU over hours.

AlexNet (2012)

INPUT: 224×224×3 5 CONV + 3 FC ~60M PARAMS 15.3% TOP-5 ERROR

AlexNet stacked the same Conv+Pool pattern from LeNet much deeper and wider, and added three critical modern techniques: ReLU activations, Dropout regularization, and GPU-accelerated training. It was the first network to prove these techniques could work together at scale on a million-image dataset.

The network was split across two GPUs due to memory constraints — each GPU handled half the filters per layer, communicating only in certain layers.

Dogs vs. Cats (2013)

25,000 IMAGES 2 CLASSES TRANSFER LEARNING >99.5% TOP ENTRIES

The top-performing entries in 2013 used a technique you will learn in Lecture 5 — Transfer Learning: take AlexNet (already trained on ImageNet), remove its final layer, and fine-tune the remaining weights on the dogs/cats dataset. The ImageNet features — which included cats, dogs, and thousands of other animals — transferred directly.

This demonstrated that a single large pre-trained network could be repurposed for new tasks in hours rather than days, with far less data — the key insight that makes modern AI practical.

What These Three Stories Teach Us

· · ·
01

Architecture is not enough. LeNet worked in 1998 but couldn't scale. The algorithm was correct; the infrastructure — compute, data, training tricks — wasn't ready.

02

GPU + Data + Depth = the trifecta. AlexNet combined all three for the first time. Remove any one of them and it fails.

03

Competitions drive progress. ImageNet and Kaggle created measurable benchmarks that forced researchers to produce verifiable, reproducible results.

04

Margins matter. A 10% improvement is interesting. AlexNet's 11-point improvement over second place was a paradigm shift. The magnitude of the gap told the community something fundamental had changed.

05

Democratization follows breakthrough. AlexNet was frontier research. Dogs vs. Cats made it accessible. This pattern — breakthrough then democratization — repeats in every wave of AI progress.

06

Transfer learning is the key. You don't need millions of images and weeks of compute. You need a good pre-trained model and a small target dataset. That's Lecture 5.

Discussion Questions
  1. LeNet achieved 99.3% on MNIST in 1998. Why did it take 14 years for CNNs to become mainstream? Which of the three barriers — compute, data, or algorithmic — do you think was most limiting?
  2. AlexNet had ~1,000× more parameters than LeNet (60M vs 60K). Does more parameters always mean better performance? What can go wrong when a network is too large for its dataset?
  3. AlexNet was trained on two GPUs because one GPU didn't have enough memory for all 60M parameters. Today, a high-end GPU has 80GB of memory. What does this tell you about the relationship between hardware progress and AI capability?
  4. The Dogs vs. Cats winners used Transfer Learning — fine-tuning an AlexNet pre-trained on ImageNet. Why would ImageNet features (1,000 categories) help with a 2-class cat/dog problem?
