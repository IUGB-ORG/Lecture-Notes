Section 1

What a Filter Actually Does

A CNN filter (or kernel) is a small matrix of learned numbers. It slides across an image and at every position computes a single number — the dot product between the filter weights and the image patch underneath. The result is a new 2-D map called a feature map or activation map.

Crucially: the same filter weights are reused at every position (weight sharing). This gives CNNs two superpowers simultaneously — the ability to detect the same pattern anywhere in the image, and an enormous reduction in parameters compared to a fully-connected layer.

// Output pixel at position (i,j) for a 3×3 filter K on image I:

O(i,j) = Σu=0..2 Σv=0..2 K(u,v) · I(i+u, j+v) + b

// This is the discrete cross-correlation operation (often loosely called convolution)

Section 2

The Hand-Crafted Era: Before Learning

For decades before CNNs, computer vision researchers designed filters by hand, encoding specific assumptions about what makes images useful to analyze. Understanding these classical filters is not just history — it directly explains why learned CNN filters look the way they do.

The Classical Filter Zoo

Why These Filters? The Core Intuitions

Intuition 1

Edges Carry the Most Information

Flat regions are uninformative — a patch of pure sky tells you nothing about content. Boundaries between regions encode shapes, objects, and layout. Sobel, Prewitt, and Laplacian filters are all designed to find exactly these transitions by measuring local differences.

Intuition 2

Noise Must Be Suppressed First

Raw pixel differences amplify sensor noise (high-frequency garbage). Gaussian smoothing removes noise before edge detection. This insight gave rise to the Canny pipeline: blur → gradient → thin → threshold — still the canonical edge detector today.

Intuition 3

Orientation Selectivity

Horizontal and vertical filters detect their respective edges. But real objects have edges at all angles. Gabor filters extend this to arbitrary orientations — they model the response of neurons in the primary visual cortex (V1), which are themselves tuned to specific orientations and spatial frequencies.

Intuition 4

Multiple Scales

Fine textures need small filters; large blobs need wide ones. Classical systems like SIFT and HOG explicitly operate at multiple scales. This insight directly motivated the Inception module (1×1, 3×3, 5×5 in parallel) and multi-scale feature pyramids in modern CNNs.

Key Insight: Every classical filter was an explicit hypothesis about what structure matters in images. CNNs don't abandon these hypotheses — they discover them automatically from data. First-layer CNN filters routinely converge to Gabor-like patterns identical to what neuroscientists and engineers had constructed by hand.

Section 3

The Revolution: Learning Filters from Data

The fundamental question shift: instead of "what filter should detect edges?" ask "what filter, if I backpropagate from the loss, does the data say I should use?". This is the CNN revolution — not a new architecture idea, but a new epistemology about where knowledge comes from.

1980 — Fukushima
Neocognitron
Hierarchical model with simple-cell and complex-cell layers, inspired by Hubel & Wiesel's visual cortex research. Filters were still biologically-inspired, hand-initialized — but the hierarchical structure was the seed of CNNs.
1989–1998 — LeCun et al.
LeNet: Filters Learn from Backprop
LeNet-5 trained 5×5 filters end-to-end via gradient descent on MNIST. The filters were initialized randomly and converged to edge/curve detectors. First proof that learned filters could outperform hand-crafted ones on a real task.
2012 — Krizhevsky, Sutskever, Hinton
AlexNet: Scale Changes Everything
GPU training on ImageNet with 11×11, 5×5, 3×3 filters. When researchers visualized the first-layer filters, they looked strikingly like Gabor filters — oriented wavelets at multiple frequencies. The network had rediscovered classical CV from first principles, from 1.2 million images.
2014 — Simonyan, Zisserman
VGGNet: The 3×3 Doctrine
Two stacked 3×3 filters have the same receptive field as one 5×5 filter — but fewer parameters and an extra nonlinearity. VGG showed you could build very deep nets from nothing but 3×3 filters. A design principle that persists to this day.
2014 — Szegedy et al. (Google)
Inception: Parallel Multi-Scale Filters
Rather than choosing a filter size, use them all at once: 1×1, 3×3, 5×5 in parallel, concatenate results. The 1×1 filter emerges as a "channel mixer" — dimensionality reduction across depth without spatial mixing. A profound new primitive.
2015 — He et al. (Microsoft)
ResNet: Bottleneck Filters + Residuals
1×1 → 3×3 → 1×1 bottleneck blocks. The outer 1×1 filters reduce and restore channels; the 3×3 does the spatial work in a compressed space. Scaled to 152 layers. Filters become increasingly abstract at depth — from edges to textures to object parts to full objects.
2017 — Howard et al. (Google)
MobileNet: Depthwise Separable Filters
Factorize a standard convolution into a depthwise filter (spatial, per-channel) + a pointwise 1×1 filter (channel mixing). Same expressiveness, ~8–9× fewer multiply-adds. The dominant approach in efficient mobile vision today.
Section 4

The 3×3 Principle: Why Small Beats Large

One of the most important lessons in CNN filter design: a stack of small filters beats a single large filter. This seems counterintuitive at first — a 7×7 filter should "see more," right? The math tells a different story.

Receptive Field Math

The receptive field of a neuron is the region of the input image it can "see" (directly or indirectly). Consider these two alternatives for achieving a 5×5 effective receptive field:

Option A — Single 5×5 Filter

One layer, big kernel

Params (one filter): 5×5×C_in = 25·C_in
Nonlinearities: 1 ReLU
Receptive field: 5×5
Option B — Two 3×3 Filters (VGG)

Two layers, small kernels

Params (two filters): 2×3×3×C_in = 18·C_in
Nonlinearities: 2 ReLUs → more expressiveness
Receptive field: still 5×5 ✓
VGG's insight: Option B uses 28% fewer parameters than Option A and has two nonlinearities instead of one, making it strictly more powerful. The same logic extends: three 3×3 layers = one 7×7 receptive field, with 46% parameter savings.
Receptive Field Growth: Stacking 3×3 Filters
Each 3×3 layer (blue border) expands the effective receptive field (shaded region) on the original input by 2px in each direction

But Not Always: When Bigger Filters Help

First Layer Exception

Large kernels at the input

AlexNet uses 11×11 in the first layer; ResNet uses 7×7. Why? The first layer operates on raw pixels, which have high spatial redundancy. A large filter quickly expands the receptive field and reduces spatial dimensions early, making subsequent layers cheaper. After the first layer, 3×3 takes over.

Long-Range Dependencies

Dilated / Atrous convolutions

A dilated 3×3 with rate r inserts gaps between filter positions, achieving a (2r+1)×(2r+1) receptive field without extra params. Used heavily in semantic segmentation (DeepLab) and audio (WaveNet) where long-range context is critical without losing resolution.

Section 5

The 1×1 Filter: The Most Underrated Primitive

At first glance, a 1×1 convolution seems useless — it only looks at a single pixel. But it operates across all channels simultaneously, making it a learned linear combination of feature maps. This is genuinely powerful.

// 1×1 conv with 256 input channels → 64 output channels
Input: H × W × 256
Filter: 1 × 1 × 256 (64 such filters)
Output: H × W × 64 ← same spatial size, fewer channels

// This is a learned dimensionality reduction — a per-pixel fully-connected layer
Use Case 1

Channel Reduction

In Inception/ResNet bottlenecks: reduce 256→64 channels before an expensive 3×3, then expand 64→256 after. The 3×3 now processes 16× fewer values. Computational savings: ~8–16×.

Use Case 2

Channel Mixing

Different feature maps often have redundant or complementary information. A 1×1 conv learns which combinations are useful — equivalent to mixing color channels to find the most discriminative signal.

Use Case 3

Nonlinear Projection

1×1 + ReLU adds a nonlinearity across channels without touching spatial structure. Used in MobileNet, SqueezeNet, and ShuffleNet to add expressiveness cheaply.

Network-in-Network (Lin et al., 2013) first formalized this idea: replace each filter's output with a small MLP applied per-position. The 1×1 convolution is the efficient special case that everyone adopted.

Section 8

What Filters Look Like at Depth

First-layer filters are interpretable — they look like Gabors and color blobs. But what do deep-layer filters detect? This is where things get fascinating and philosophically interesting.

Layer 1 — First Convolution
Edges, Colors, Orientations
Gabor-like oriented edge detectors, color opponent pairs (R–G, B–Y, luminance), and isotropic blobs. Visually interpretable. Nearly identical across different networks trained on ImageNet.
Layers 2–4 — Early-Mid
Textures, Gratings, Curves
Combinations of edges: textures (brick, fur, fabric), curved contours, junctions and corners. Still somewhat interpretable. Neuroscience parallel: V2/V4 cortical areas responding to texture and curvature.
Layers 5–8 — Mid-Deep
Object Parts, Patterns
Wheel detectors, face detectors, text detectors. Neurons respond to complex multi-part patterns. Feature visualization (maximally activating inputs) reveals recognizable object components. Transfer learning works here because these are general visual parts.
Final Layers
Semantic Concepts
Full-object detectors: "dog snout", "car door", "human eye". The filter is now a complex function over thousands of pixels but can be described in human terms. Saliency maps and Grad-CAM visualize which spatial regions are driving these neurons.
Universal Visual Feature Hierarchy (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Yosinski et al., 2015): Networks trained on ImageNet learn a hierarchy that transfers remarkably well to other vision tasks. The lower layers are nearly universal — fine-tuning only the last few layers often outperforms training from scratch on small datasets.

Section 9

General Principles for Choosing Filters Today

Drawing on everything learned from the classical era through modern architectures, here is the consolidated wisdom on filter selection:

Principle 1

Start with 3×3. Always.

Unless you have a strong reason (e.g., first layer on high-res input), 3×3 is the right default. Two stacked 3×3 layers achieve 5×5 receptive field with fewer params and more expressiveness. Three achieve 7×7. VGG proved this in 2014; every modern architecture agrees.

Principle 2

Use 1×1 for channel surgery

Whenever you need to change the number of channels — up or down — use a 1×1 conv. It's the cheapest way to add a nonlinear cross-channel projection. Use it as a bottleneck before expensive spatial convolutions, and as an expander after depthwise convolutions.

Principle 3

More filters ≠ better

Filter counts should grow as spatial resolution shrinks (standard: double channels when halving H×W). But adding more filters to a fixed spatial scale increases computation quadratically. Prefer depth (more layers) over width (more filters per layer) in most regimes.

Principle 4

On mobile: depthwise separable

Replace every 3×3 conv with a depthwise 3×3 + pointwise 1×1. Reduces FLOPs by ~8–9× with minimal accuracy loss. MobileNet, EfficientNet, and virtually every on-device model uses this. Non-negotiable for edge deployment.

Principle 5

Let the data decide

Don't pre-initialize filters with Sobel or Gabor kernels (unless you have very little data). Xavier/He initialization + gradient descent will find better filters for your specific task than any hand-crafted alternative. The data knows what features matter; your job is to provide the architecture for them to emerge.

Principle 6

NAS validates intuitions

Neural Architecture Search (EfficientNet, NASNet) uses gradient-free or differentiable search to find optimal filter counts, sizes, and layer combinations. The result? EfficientNet looks like a refined MobileNet. NASNet cells look like Inception-ResNet hybrids. Human intuitions were largely correct.

Summary mantra: 3×3 spatial · 1×1 channel · more depth than width · depthwise on edge · learn from data · trust NAS to validate.
